Moonlight Sonata - Ludwig van Beethoven
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

Tabulated from the (much easier to play) piano score by David Atkinson
October 1994 - V2.0 (11/94)

I have included left hand fingering underneath the tab.  I think the system
I used is self-explanitary.  The numbers 1 to 4 indicate the fingers -
1=index, 2=middle, 3=ring, 4=little(pinkie).  I have left out fingering where
a section is repeated or on parts where fingering is blatantly obvious.
If anyone needs an explanation, feel free to email me.

I transposed this to the key of A minor which seemed logical to me at the
time but I have recently been informed that Tarrega used D minor. Tough!
I experimented with the B string tuned to Bb but, although some parts were
easier, most were better with normal tuning.  Any other suggestions?

I might just try a D minor version if I find the time and motivation.
Watch this space!

p = pull-off, h = hammer-on, s = slide

===== = sustain.  However, to save me hassle, sustain all the bass notes
for a whole bar where possible.

The roman numerals above the tab indicate either full barres or half
barres.  The ...s indicate how long the barre lasts.

                              Moonlight sonata
                                  Beethoven

    Piano Sonata No 14 "Quasi una fantasia" (Italian: In the manner of a fantasy)
                                1st movement

Beethoven wrote his Piano Sonata No. 14 in 1801 and dedicated it to
17-year-old Countess Giulietta Guicciardi, with whom he was deeply in love.
Its original title, "Quasi una Fantasia", was in keeping with that dedication.
However, in 1832, the poet Ludwig Rellstab compared the music to "moonlight
shining on a lake." Since then, it became known as the Moonlight Sonata.

Its powerful, haunting and quiet melody was at first disturbing to audiences.
However, Beethoven later complained of its popularity, writing,
"Surely I've written better things."

Standard Tuning; 4/4, in Am minor  (composed in C sharp minor)

(All but the last two bars are in triplet 8ths, as noted in bar #1. With
several bars ending in 16ths; These notes are shown as "s" above the note).
Also, there are numerous dynamics regarding tempo and note accents,
far too many to note without making the tab look cluttered. My best advice
would be to find and listen to a recording.

 left hand   comfortable with some of them, don't hesitate to try other ways
 1 - index   of freting these cords. For example in the first measure (Am) I
 2 - middle  exchange my middle and ring fingers for easier transition into
 3 - ring    the F shape of bar three.
 4 - little

 Let ring throughout (wherever possable)

I've have used this site so extensively that I thought it was time I returned
something. Beethoven being my favourite composer, I thought this would be
appropriate (Moonlight Sonata 1st Movement). I added some LHF but please
remember its the LHF that I use and will probably need some adjustments. Also
there are some pretty tricky parts in the song which should be practiced
specificaly, because the chord change will take about 200 practices before you
can do it smoothly. Moonlight Sonata has already been tabbed but my version has
a crescendo that is an octave higher and that makes full use of the guitar's
fretboard. All the notes are 1/8th triplets in 4/4 time except the ones marked
S=16th note, H=Half, F=Full. THeres a tricky rythym that uses the 16th note in
the main body, it probably best to listen to the midi file to understand it
properly. Other than that Enjoy. - s_ghyselincks@hotmail.com - February 2005
