Pavane pour une Infante defunte (Pavane for a Dead Princess)
by Maurice Ravel (1875-1937)

The French composer, Maurice Ravel, claimed to have chosen the title of
his "Pavane pour une infante defunte" on grounds of euphony [how well it
sounded] rather than for any other reason. Written for piano in 1899, This
piece won immediate popularity in its original form, extended by the later
orchestral version made by the composer. Whether the nostalgic music, based
on an old dance form, mourns a Spanish princess or not, it has about it a
characteristic fin de siecle air of yearning for an unattainable and ideal
past, a quality Ravel shared with his teacher Gabriel Faure.

[Ravel wrote "It is not a lament for a dead child, but an evocation of the
pavane which might have been danced by a tiny princess such as was painted
by Velasquez at the Spanish Court" - Weed, 17 Nov 2000]
